## Outline

### H1: How the FCC 1:1 Consent Rule Changes Lead Buying (And What It Means for You)

### H2: What Is the FCC 1:1 Consent Rule?
- Plain-English explanation: consumers must now consent to be contacted by EACH specific company, not just "marketing partners" broadly
- Previous rule: consumer fills out form, checks one box, gets sold to 5-10 companies
- New rule: consumer must specifically consent to EACH company that will contact them
- Effective date and enforcement timeline
- Why it was created: consumer protection, reducing unwanted calls

### H2: What This Means for Lead Buyers
- **Shared leads become more expensive** — vendors need separate consent for each buyer
- **Exclusive leads gain value** — one-to-one consent aligns perfectly with exclusive model
- **Aged leads are affected differently** — leads generated BEFORE the rule may still have broad consent
- **Your vendor matters more than ever** — compliance starts at the source

### H2: How This Affects Different Industries

#### H3: Insurance Lead Buying
- Impact on shared health/life/auto leads
- How carriers and agencies need to adapt
- ACA/Medicare implications during enrollment periods

#### H3: Mortgage Lead Buying
- Impact on comparison/rate table leads
- How LOs and brokers are affected
- Already a heavily regulated space — layering on top of TILA/RESPA

#### H3: Solar and Home Improvement
- Similar impact on comparison leads
- Less regulatory complexity than insurance/mortgage

### H2: What Doesn't Change
- The aged lead model itself is still valid
- Buying leads is still legal and effective
- Multi-channel follow-up still works
- The fundamentals of working leads are unchanged
- Clarify: this rule changes HOW leads are generated, not HOW you work them

### H2: How to Protect Yourself as a Lead Buyer
1. **Ask your vendor:** "How does your consent process comply with the 1:1 rule?"
2. **Get documentation:** request consent records and form examples
3. **Verify consent:** if the vendor can't show how consent was obtained, walk away
4. **Prefer exclusive leads** when budget allows (inherently 1:1 compliant)
5. **Understand the transition:** leads generated before the rule date may have different consent standards
6. **Stay updated:** the rule may evolve through litigation and FCC guidance

### H2: What This Means for Aged Leads Specifically
- Aged leads generated BEFORE the rule: existing consent may be broader (under old rules)
- Aged leads generated AFTER the rule: should have specific 1:1 consent
- Ask your vendor about the generation date AND consent type for aged lead batches
- Bottom line: aged leads remain viable, but data provenance matters more

### H2: The Silver Lining — Why This Could Be Good for Smart Lead Buyers
- Less competition on each lead (fewer companies per consent)
- Higher quality leads (consumers who specifically chose to hear from you)
- Potential for higher conversion rates (warmer consent = warmer lead)
- Agents with good systems win in a more regulated environment (the amateurs drop out)

### H2: FAQ
1. **Does the FCC 1:1 consent rule kill lead buying?** [No — it changes how leads are generated, not whether you can buy them]
2. **Do I need to change how I work my existing leads?** [No — the rule affects lead generation consent, not your follow-up process]
3. **Are aged leads still compliant?** [Yes — especially those generated before the rule, and those from compliant sources after]
4. **Should I switch from shared to exclusive leads?** [Exclusive aligns better with 1:1 consent, but shared can still be compliant if vendor is properly consented]
5. **What should I ask my lead vendor?** [How they obtain consent, whether it's 1:1 or broad, and if they have documentation]
